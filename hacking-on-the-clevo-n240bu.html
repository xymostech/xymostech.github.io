Hacking on the Clevo N240BU

Written December 22, 2016

I recently bought a new computer, the Clevo N240BU. You might have never heard of Clevo computers (or at least I never had) but you've probably seen some of their computers rebranded and sold under other name like Sager or System76. In fact, the way I found out about their computers was by looking for specs for a System76 laptop. I'm gonna walk through the process I went through of deciding to buy one of these laptops and maybe some helpful tips for anyone else who wants to do something similar.

To a person like me, the System76 Lemur is a pretty sweet looking laptop. It's pretty small (14" screen, less than 1" thick), can boast an i7 processor and 32 gigs of RAM, and comes with Ubuntu preinstalled. My current laptop, a Dell XPS 13, has a really wonderful build quality but it's really expensive to get one with more RAM and if you want an i7 and more RAM it's also gonna come with a touch screen, which I would rather not pay for. So, the Lemur has exactly what I want: tons of ram and a fast processor for pretty cheap and with a standard 1080p screen. Awesome.

But could I get it cheaper? I'm not afraid of taking apart my laptop, so I looked into buying my own memory, hard drive(s), and wireless card and installing it myself. A cursory glance around the internet shows that if I bought the cheap model and put my own RAM, SSD, and wifi card, I could make a computer for about $100 cheaper than what I could get on System76. I run Arch Linux so having Ubuntu preinstalled isn't helpful since I would have to do my own install anyway. This was sounding great in theory but I know I've been bitten recently by laptops that come with the RAM and SSD soldered to the motherboard. So I started doing some research about whether the System76 Lemur could be upgraded. And what did I find?

Nothing. Actually, as far as I can tell, spec sheets for the System76 Lemur can't be found. The Lemur Quick Start Guide is about as close as I could find to a manual or guide but it just comes with some outside shots of the computer and a list of the tech specs. Odd. Why would a company devoted to making computers for hackers (I guess they're trying to make Linux computers which are accessible to non-techy folks, but we all know that people buying Linux computers are more likely to be able to take apart their computers) not provide any documentation? With some more searching I eventually stumbled upon the name of a Taiwanese computer company named "Clevo" which led me to their website which led me to the discovery of the N240BU.

For the most part, this made things click for me. System76 doesn't seem like a large enough organization to be actually manufacturing laptops from scratch so it makes sense that they're just rebranding a different company's laptop. The value they provide is in making sure all of the parts actually work with Linux and doing the Ubuntu pre-install. That also maybe explains why the computer has such an odd selection of ports (who needs a VGA port on their laptop? Or an Ethernet port? Actually an Ethernet port sounds pretty handy sometimes) instead of trying to go the Mac-esque direction with everything being sleek. And lastly, it explains why there are no product manuals for the Lemur: I was searching for the wrong product!

Well, it sorta explains that last point. It probably wouldn't be too hard for System76 to provide a link to the real product manuals on their website. Maybe they don't want people finding out the truth about their computers (which isn't that big of a deal). Or maybe it's because even with the correct product ID, there still aren't very good manuals for this computer! The downloads section on the Clevo website gives you something, but it's only long because it's translated into a million languages. The only shot of the inside is when they show you where to put your SIM card in case you get the optional 3G chip. Would it kill you to make a repair guide for how to access the different parts? Was this a sign that the parts actually weren't replaceable, which is why they don't bother telling you how to open the thing up?

So finding out this hidden secret wasn't helping out with figuring out whether I can upgrade the parts myself. Maybe it could help in a different way? Thus, I stumbled upon R&J Tech, which sells barebone Clevo laptops, including our favourite Clevo N240BU, without RAM, hard drives, Wi-Fi cards, or an OS! Leaving those out drops the price of the laptop down another $150, making this look like an even better option. And the fact that they're selling these computers provides some pretty good evidence that the end consumer would be able to plug in their own parts.

But pretty good doesn't mean that it's 100% likely. So what's the solution in this case? It's pretty obvious: buy the cheap laptop and find out for myself if I can replace the parts. Then, armed with my new knowledge, take a whole bunch of pictures and post about it on the internet! And if it doesn't work out, and the weird barebone computer doesn't actually let me put anything in it, then it'll be extra funny. Plus, there's a return policy.
